Research

Background

My primary interests have been in developing new statistical and computational techniques to enable the analysis of high-dimensional astrophysical data. This includes image processing algorithms at the pixel level, through filtering of data streams for real-time analysis, to retrospective analyses of large-scale databases for trends and outliers. This has been done in collaboration with hundreds of scientists from around the world, and has occurred at the boundary of big data / big compute in the field of astronomy, from its inception in the mid-1990s to developing future surveys that will see first light in 2010.

Research Interests

  • Dark Matter
    I have participated in several investigations into the nature and extent of the dark matter that composes 27% of the total mass of our Universe. The first was the focus of my thesis work with the MACHO project. Our challenge was to filter 10^7 lightcurves to find rare microlensing events that numbered in the tens (Alcock et al., 2000). We were one of the first wide-field surveys that reduced our data in real-time, which began in earnest the era of "big-data" in Astronomy, where "big" means an accumulated database of 200 Gb in the year 2000. I have since participated in the next generation Super-MACHO microlensing experiment, and supernova cosmology experiments that also constrain the dark matter content of the Universe.
  • Dark Energy
    Supernova cosmology experiments constrain both the dark matter and dark energy content of the Universe, the latter of which lead to the 2011 Nobel Prize in Physics for two of my collaborators, Adam Riess and Brian Schmidt. I have worked on second generation supernova cosmology experiments to constrain the nature of the dark energy, in particular its equation of state, with both the ESSENCE and SDSS-II supernova surveys. In both projects, my involvement included the real-time filtering of data and visualization/validation of the real-time data stream.
  • Exoplanets
    I have recently been investigating the limits of precision in ground-based and space-based astronomy through the study of planets around other stars, a.k.a. "exoplanets". This includes ground-based observations of known exoplanet systems with the APOSTLE project, which I am the PI for. I also have been working on detrending and modeling the data from the Kepler spacecraft, which present some of the highest precision astronomical time-series that have ever been acquired.
  • Large Synoptic Survey Telescope
    I have spent the past 8 years working towards defining the scope of, and validating the algorithms behind, the Large Synoptic Survey Telescope, the future of our field. This includes creating the data inputs for, and the mining of, 4-billion row databases to assess the quality of algorithms. This project is slated to begin construction in fiscal year 2014.

Current

  • Time Domain Analytics
    My focus going forward is to hone my expertise in time domain analytics to develope a classification and predictive infrastructure for vector-valued time sequences of data. The most relevant example of this would be classifying variable stars based on their multi-passband lightcurves. But this idea is general enough that a well-designed platform will have relevance beyond the astrophysical.
